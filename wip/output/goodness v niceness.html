Goodness vs niceness

Not clear this is the right set of labels. But: goodness and niceness increasingly seem a bit in tension (or at least, not the same thing, I have limited time, etc). What should I do about that?

Concretely:

  • Niceness is something like: local goodness, helping the community. Strawmanned: ineffective altruism. Also: cooking meals for your housemates, having slack in your schedule to enable being a back up person, random spontaneous nice things for those round you. Small scale supererogatory reliability. Baking cakes for the christmas fete.

  • Goodness is: actually trying, scope sensitivity. Working long hours, impact maximising, setting aside personal interests. Strawmanned: cold and heartless mechanical moves. But it usually involves working hard, optimising your schedule, fitting stuff in. Impact focus. You’re a vessel for good acts to be caused through.

  • And virtue is a third thing. One can be virtuous and good, and unvirtuous and nice (though lots of niceness is in virtues). But I mean that e.g. reliability and consistency are not just properties of niceness, but also core to goodness. (And goodness shouldn’t make you a shitty housemate.)

I suppose some questions are: how do you split your time between both? (But I know the answer and think the rest is hand-wringing: you satisfice on niceness, and then aim hard for goodness.) Another question: the character traits both require are different; which should you cultivate? (But that’s the same question in disguise.)

I think I'm feeling bad about not being nicer, even though I do think niceness is supererogatory. (Though maybe my feelings are because I'm worried that I drew the "supererogatory" boundary wrong, and so am worried I'm shirking on the required amount of niceness.)

The core struggle appears to be finding a way to honor both analytical reasoning and personal feeling without falling into self-deception or complete emotional capitulation. It's a delicate balance between critical thinking and emotional intelligence, where the user wants to be simultaneously clear-eyed and compassionate toward their own inner experience. The tension arises from an impossible ethical calculus that demands simultaneous commitment and detachment. Impact-driven thinking creates a paradoxical mandate: sacrifice deeply while remaining emotionally unscathed.

Potential resolution strategies emerge from challenging core assumptions. The framework might misframe legitimate values as peripheral, when they could be integral to meaningful action. Personal preferences aren't necessarily obstacles to impact, but potentially essential components of sustainable ethical engagement.

The core insight involves recognizing that impact maximization isn't a rigid external mandate, but a chosen approach with negotiable boundaries. Guilt and internal conflict might signal unacknowledged deeper values being suppressed in pursuit of a narrowly defined effectiveness.

Some patterns I notice:

  1. The same structure keeps appearing

In this piece: "View 2 is closer to the truth, but I feel pulled to View 1, and I'm suspicious of that pull as cope, but also suspicious of dismissing it too fast."

In the niceness/goodness thing: "Goodness is what actually matters, but I feel bad about not being nicer, and I'm not sure if that feeling is tracking something real or is just noise."

In the values change discussion: "Reflection and consistency are good, but maybe cope is protecting something real."

The structure is: hard-nosed view is endorsed, softer pull exists, suspicion of the pull, but also suspicion of the suspicion.

  1. "Cope" is load-bearing in a weird way

You treat "motivated reasoning / cope" as a constant threat to be vigilant against. But you also keep noting that it might be protecting something real. You never quite resolve what the status of that is.

Possible unstated premise: the burden of proof is always on the softer option. Hard-nosed is default-true; any departure is suspect until proven innocent.

Is that actually your view? It would explain the guilt structure—you feel bad when you deviate from the hard-nosed view because deviation requires justification you haven't provided.

  1. The guilt is doing something you haven't examined

You say you don't believe you should feel vaguely bad all the time. But you also seem to think that not feeling bad might indicate you're not taking things seriously. This is an unstable equilibrium—what's the right amount of bad feeling?

Possible unstated premise: guilt is epistemically relevant. If you don't feel bad about a tradeoff, maybe you're coping. So you need to maintain some guilt to stay honest.

This would explain the "I know it's supererogatory but I feel bad anyway" thing. The guilt isn't a mistake; it's functioning as a check against self-deception.

  1. Something about permission

The "voyage" paragraph at the end of your piece is striking:

"the way it feels when you're awake and alive and can do anything. Embarking on a voyage, with something like honesty in your heart."

This is a completely different register from the EV-maximizing frame. It's almost romantic. And you put it under "feeling more reflective and/or full of cope"—you can't quite own it.

Possible thing you want but haven't given yourself permission for: a life that feels like a voyage, not an optimization problem. And you're not sure that's allowed.

  1. The "supererogatory" move might be doing too much work

Labeling niceness as supererogatory lets you:

  • Not do it (it's not obligatory)

  • Feel bad about not doing it (it's still good)

  • Not update your self-concept (you're still a good person who happens to not be doing this optional thing)

But maybe the category is wrong. Maybe niceness isn't supererogatory but constitutive of a life you actually want—which means not doing it isn't "forgoing the optional" but "failing at something you care about."

That would explain the bad feeling better than "I correctly identified it as supererogatory but have irrational emotions."

Potential reading

Iris Murdoch, "The Sovereignty of Good"

She's against exactly the kind of moral philosophy that builds castles. She thinks moral life is about attention—seeing reality clearly, which the ego constantly distorts. The enemy isn't wrong principles; it's the self getting in the way of seeing. And she's suspicious of the will-focused, decision-focused framing. Might offer a way to be morally serious that isn't IM.

Simone Weil, "Gravity and Grace"

Intensely morally serious, but the mode is receptivity and attention, not optimization. She writes about decreation—getting the self out of the way. It's demanding but not in the "suck it up and maximize" way. More like: become capable of seeing. Fragmentary, aphoristic, hits differently than argumentative philosophy.

Rilke, "Letters to a Young Poet"

On living the questions. On not resolving too fast. On patience with the unresolved. Might speak to the "becoming legible too fast" worry directly.

"Be patient toward all that is unsolved in your heart and try to love the questions themselves."

Kierkegaard, "Fear and Trembling"

The knight of faith looks from the outside like an ordinary bourgeois, but internally is living something absurd and total. Contrasted with the "knight of infinite resignation" who gives things up and knows he's given them up. Might resonate with the voyage-feeling-false problem—Kierkegaard thinks you can't get there by reasoning, only by a leap. Whether that's cope or something else is the question.

Bernard Williams, "Ethics and the Limits of Philosophy"

Rigorous philosophical argument against systematization in ethics. He thinks the demand for consistency and theory is itself a mistake. "Morality" as a system is a peculiar historical invention, and living well might require less coherence than philosophers want. Would give you ammunition if you decide consistency isn't a desideratum.

Dostoevsky, "The Brothers Karamazov"

Ivan builds the logical castle. His critique is airtight. And it breaks him. Alyosha has no counter-argument; he kisses Ivan. The novel doesn't resolve who's right—but it shows what living in the castle does to you.

Also: the Grand Inquisitor chapter specifically, on what it means to take responsibility for people vs. respecting their freedom.

Zbigniew Herbert (since you mentioned)

If you haven't read widely in him beyond what Joe cites: "The Envoy of Mr. Cogito," "Report from the Besieged City," "A Knocker." He's about maintaining dignity and moral seriousness without fanaticism, without certainty, amid ruin.

Czesław Miłosz, "The Captive Mind"

On how intelligent people convince themselves. On the phenomenology of adopting an ideology—how it feels from the inside, how the cope becomes invisible. Might be useful for the "how do I know when I'm coping" question.

Annie Dillard, "Pilgrim at Tinker Creek"

If you want to be blown open rather than argued into a position. She's just looking at things—nature, light, violence, fecundity—with an intensity that's religious without being systematic. A different mode of attention.

Marilynne Robinson, "Gilead"

A dying pastor writing to his young son. Morally serious, gentle, not optimizing. Shows what it looks like to live in uncertainty with grace. Not a philosophical argument—a depiction.

Less obvious:

  • Larkin, "Aubade": Unflinching about terror, no consolation, but also no fake hardness. Just looking at the thing.

  • Cavell, "Must We Mean What We Say?": On the ordinary, on skepticism, on what acknowledgment requires. Difficult but might land.

  • Camus, "The Myth of Sisyphus": On living without resolution, without appeal, without the leap. "One must imagine Sisyphus happy."

Let me try.

Jan Zwicky, "Wisdom & Metaphor"

Not an argument but a composition—aphorisms and images on facing pages, meant to be read sideways. Her claim: systematic thinking (she calls it "calculative") can't reach certain things that matter. Lyric can. This isn't a rejection of rigor; she's a logician. It's a claim that some understanding is structured differently. Might speak to the castles-in-the-sky problem without just saying "stop building castles."

Gillian Rose, "Love's Work"

A philosopher dying of cancer. Refuses consolation and refuses despair. Writes about the "broken middle"—the place between system and chaos where you actually have to live. She's against premature resolution in either direction. Dense, strange, not comforting. The line people quote: "Keep your mind in hell, and despair not."

Stanley Cavell, "Conditions Handsome and Unhandsome"

His version of moral perfectionism. Ethics isn't about principles you apply; it's about a "next self" you're drawn toward, who you're not yet. The Emersonian idea that conformity is the enemy, including conformity to your own past. Might reframe values change: not "should I trust the output" but "what's the relationship between who I am and who I'm becoming."

James C. Scott, "Seeing Like a State"

Not ethics, but: the book is about how legibility is a tool of control. States need to make populations legible to govern them. What resists legibility often has functional value the system can't see. The examples are forests and cities but the frame transfers. Your worry about "becoming coherent too fast" might be a version of this—self-legibility as a kind of violence against the parts of you that function illegibly.

D.W. Winnicott, "Playing and Reality"

Psychoanalyst, but not in the annoying way. He writes about the "true self" vs "false self"—the false self is compliant, adaptive, built to manage the environment. The true self is... harder to say. It's what plays. When you say romanticizing feels false, Winnicott might say: of course it does, you're trying to adopt it as a position, but it can't be adopted, only emerged-into.

Anne Carson, "Decreation"

Essays and poetry on Simone Weil, Sappho, others. The title essay is about "undoing" the self—not destruction but a kind of clearing. Less systematic than Weil herself, more oblique. The form does something the content doesn't say.

R.S. Thomas

Welsh priest, wrote poems for fifty years about doubt, absence, the deus absconditus. Morally serious without resolution. No comfort, but not grim in the IM way—more like weathered.

"It is this great absence / that is like a presence, that compels / me to address it without hope / of a survey before me."

Ursula K. Le Guin, "The Lathe of Heaven"

Novel about a man whose dreams change reality. He doesn't want this power. A utilitarian doctor tries to use him to improve the world. It goes badly, in ways that are about the structure of optimization, not just execution errors. Short, readable, might hit the "god of impact eating its young" thing from an angle.

Geoffrey Hill, especially "The Triumph of Love"

Difficult, crabbed, morally obsessed. He's working through guilt, complicity, history, conscience—not resolving it, just staying with it. Not pleasant. But there's a particular texture of moral seriousness that isn't the EA texture, and he has it.

Harry Frankfurt, "The Reasons of Love"

Not the bullshit essay. This one argues that love creates reasons rather than responding to them. What you care about isn't justified by something deeper; it's just what you care about, and that's where justification bottoms out. Might help with the "which pulls should I defer to" question—maybe the question is malformed.

The concept of acedia (the "noonday demon")—a monastic term for a specific kind of spiritual listlessness that's different from depression or laziness. There's a book by Kathleen Norris called "Acedia & Me." Might name something about the "grim and ugh-y" feeling that's not quite captured by "burnout."

Tolstoy, "A Confession"

This is very close to what you're describing. He'd achieved everything, reasoned through all the positions, and found himself suicidal—not from despair but from logical consistency. The book is him trying to figure out how the peasants can live and he can't. He doesn't resolve it cleanly. He decides reasoning itself was the trap, but he's not sure he believes that either.

Mill, "Autobiography" (especially chapters 5-7)

The breakdown chapter. He was raised as a pure reasoning machine. It broke him. He recovered through poetry and feeling—but he's a philosopher, so he's also trying to figure out what that means. He doesn't repudiate reason; he's trying to find its proper place.

Pascal, "Pensées"

Fragments, not a system. He was a mathematician and scientist who became convinced that reason leads you to the edge and then you need something else. "The heart has its reasons that reason does not know." But he's not anti-rational—he's a genius reasoner who thinks reasoning shows you its own limits.

On moral demandingness, lived:

Dorothy Day, "The Long Loneliness"

She actually did it—voluntary poverty, Catholic Worker houses, decades of direct service. The autobiography is about what that life was like from the inside. Not triumphant. She's honest about failure, difficulty, the gap between aspiration and reality. And she's not telling you to do it; she's just telling you what she did.

Tolstoy's later diaries

After his conversion he tried to live by his principles—gave away wealth, worked in the fields, renounced his copyrights. He kept failing. His wife fought him. He was miserable. The diaries are the record of someone trying to take moral demandingness with total seriousness and not managing it.

Thomas Merton, "The Sign of Jonas" and other journals

A monk. The journals are about contemplation and action, withdrawal and engagement, what you owe the world vs. what you owe God. He never resolves it; he keeps circling.

Dietrich Bonhoeffer, "Letters and Papers from Prison"

He was a pacifist theologian who joined a plot to kill Hitler. The letters are him working out what that means—when do your principles give way? What's left? Written knowing he'd probably be executed.

On not knowing what you value:

Montaigne, "Essays"

The original. He's explicitly not building a system. He's watching himself, catching himself in contradictions, trying to be honest about what he actually does vs. what he says. "Que sais-je?"—what do I know? Including about myself.

"I have seen no more evident monstrosity and miracle in the world than myself."

Iris Murdoch's novels (not her philosophy)

She dramatizes self-deception better than almost anyone. Characters who think they're acting from good motives and aren't. The Gap between the story you tell yourself and what's actually happening. "The Black Prince," "The Sea, The Sea," "A Severed Head."

Penelope Fitzgerald

Slantwise, less obvious. Her novels are about people who are slightly opaque to themselves. Nothing dramatic—just the ordinary way people don't quite know why they do things. "The Blue Flower," "The Gate of Angels."

On the system-building trap:

Kierkegaard, "Concluding Unscientific Postscript"

A long book against Hegel, against system-building, written under a pseudonym who's a comic figure. The form is the argument—you can't capture existence in a system; the attempt is absurd. But it's also very funny?

Wittgenstein's "Culture and Value"

Notebook entries. He keeps returning to the limits of what philosophy can do. "Philosophy leaves everything as it is." He's not anti-philosophical; he did philosophy obsessively. But he thought it was a kind of therapy that should end.

Simone Weil, "Gravity and Grace" and notebooks

Fragments. She resists systematization while being intensely rigorous. She was a philosopher but distrusted philosophy. "Attention, taken to its highest degree, is the same thing as prayer."

Poetry:

George Herbert, "The Temple"

17th-century devotional poetry. He wants to be good and can't. The poems are about the gap. "Love bade me welcome; yet my soul drew back." Plain language, formal structures, genuine struggle.

Gerard Manley Hopkins, the "terrible sonnets"

A priest in desolation. "I wake and feel the fell of dark, not day." He's not performing suffering; he's in it.

T.S. Eliot, "Four Quartets"

The attempt to arrive somewhere after reasoning fails. "In order to arrive at what you do not know / You must go by a way which is the way of ignorance." Not easy but maybe the most sustained attempt in English poetry to get at this.

Philip Larkin

The opposite move—deflationary, skeptical, refusing consolation. Might be useful as a counter-voice. "Aubade" is about facing death without any framework. "This is what we fear—no sight, no sound, / No touch or taste or smell, nothing to think with, / Nothing to love or link with."
