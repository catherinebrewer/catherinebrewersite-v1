what's going on with this values change thing

Circling some basic confusions:

  • A bunch of ethics bottoms out in “do what the idealised version of you would do”
  • And many people’s hopes for the long term future is “do a ton of reflection, get a ton of advice, not only get corrected on epistemic errors but also values, such that we act on better values in the end”
  • Moral progress to date seems good to me
  • But I have an intuitive mistrust of idealised me
  • And if I could wave a wand right now and become who I’ll be after my values change, I’m not sure I would
  • (And this is somewhat common – cf discussion of values drift over time within EA)
  • (And I can’t clearly price in values change into EV-based decision theories, if the values change is sufficiently basic/fundamental, or if the experience is otherwise transformative..?)

(What are my actual questions? Some attempts:)

  • How do I know if values change was good?
  • By whose lights?
  • How would I become confident enough to step into the values-change machine/take the wise AI advisor’s advice?
  • In some sense, I think this values change will be good. (E.g., I would rather humanity do a ton of moral reflection and learning and so on before making irreversible decisions). So why am I squeamish about it now?
  • Both in terms of “if I could replace my values”, and also in mundane ways.
  • If I generally think the long term arc of values change bends towards improvement, then I shouldn’t be doing stuff like “precommitting to donate all my money”, etc. (And if I think that’s true for other people, then they shouldn’t do that, EA shouldn’t worry about value drift, etc.)
  • How do you think sensibly about fundamental changes in your values, actually?
  • It seems easier the more consequentialist/denying-separateness-of-persons you are, and relatedly the more Parfitian you are.
  • Because then you’re more of a vector for goodness to flow through, and personal identity is relatively more dissolved for you. Glass tunnels, etc.

Some disentangling

  • Values change: one possible disentangling
  • Epistemic correction. Lots of supposed values differences are just differences in beliefs about the world. E.g., I learn that factory farms have different practices than I expected → I “care more” about animal welfare
    • (well, more precisely, here I claim people may put the same moral weights on animal welfare as before, but make an epistemic update, and so their behaviour looks like they made a moral change.).
  • Conceptual clarification. I’m not yet convinced this is a distinct thing, but, e.g. – you care about some X, say “sentience” or “wellbeing”. You later learn that you were confused about the extension or reference of that X, and so what-you-value shifts.
    • And in principle this would include dissolving caring, e.g. upon understanding what “sentience” means you no longer care about sentience.
    • (I think lots of my scepticism here is: concepts aren’t “out there”, they’re not natural kinds we have no say in, we decide what they refer to and their extension; it feels like the move of, noticing that the conceptual extension, say, is different than you thought and then updating your moral beliefs/desires is the wrong way round, the concepts you choose to care about should be responsive to your moral values and not vice versa. And even if one can’t change a moral concept in isolation, because language is social, you just take a different concept as the thing you care about, then. (“I care about robust agency!”, the philosopher cries.))
  • Deep values change. (Or fundamental, higher, etc etc etc). Something on a basic level changes.
    • I was going to say e.g., Mill, but rereading, it’s more like a change in tactics. (Which doesn’t fit super cleanly into my distinctions. I probably need better distinctions. If anything it’d be epistemic.)
    • Maybe just: the moment when, as a child, you realise that other people have their own lives, too. Or some kinds of political changes seem downstream of this? Unclear.
    • And obviously ultimately on a spectrum with shallow values change, below.
  • Shallow values change. From personal taste (you like jazz now), smaller moral changes (perhaps you get more into charity, or less into it/more into somethingelse).
  • (Where to put, e..g, people’s sympathies with first order moral theories?)

These distinctions are quite nice, and their main issue is that they’re not at all real. Indeed, it’s hard to see where any important changes fit in here. I’ll navel-gaze, for an example:

  • At some point in early childhood: realise other people have an internal life, thoughts and wants, etc.
  • At some point in later childhood: oh yeah, that probably applies to animals too, or at least, they feel pain, including non-cute animals.
  • Teenagerhood: read DGB, philosophy class, etc.
  • Actually care more (?). A slow change over time, if so.
  • Man things are way cheaper than I thought. Uh oh, moral demandingness!
  • Late teenagerhood: more moral philosophy, more meta-ethics, more EA stuff.
  • More “shut up and multiply” vibes. Shrimp welfare donations
  • Caring more about virtue and virtuous acts, mostly for instrumental reasons but at least somewhat for their own sake
  • Goodness vs niceness distinction
    • (Or local vs global goodness, whatever)
  • Something like caring more about families/letting ppl get away with lower effectiveness/valuing it more intrinsically?

Likewise:

  • Where does Mill’s breakdown (and rebuild) fit here?
  • What happens when you switch from e.g. consequentialism to virtue ethics?
  • Where does “a change in tactics” fit? And for fairly basic, fundamental differences in tactics?

  • I might just be wrong about how devastating my examples are

  • Obviously that might be cope – there might be a sense in which they don’t believe that, and they arrived at the policy first (no reading!) and then are trying to justify it in a non-evil-sounding way, minimise internal dissonance. Does that make a difference? Do I care about stated changes or something else?)
    • One answer: If most historical "moral progress" is actually empirical updates or people stopping their motivated reasoning, then maybe there's not much evidence for actual fundamental values change. And maybe the user's squeamishness is about a category that rarely actually happens.
    • But if Option C is real and common, then people do make fundamental changes, and the question of "how do I evaluate that" is more pressing.

Historically, maybe lots of the action in “recognition criteria”, e.g. when to deploy my first order values, who counts in principle, what are the outweighing reasons, what features of a situation are morally relevant. Hmm

One quick take (leaning heavily on LLMs):

  • Some examples of “genuine” values shift (e.g., honor culture -> dignity cultures; I’m not sure precisely what this is, Opus says: “Historical view: Your worth is your reputation. Insult can justify violence. Shame is about external standing. Modern view: Inherent worth independent of reputation. Violence for insult is disproportionate.”) Ditto for how views on punishment and suffering have changed.
  • Lots of examples of “some shift, then cope, then update”: slavery (need universalist values first, but jefferson is clearly full of cope).
  • Some examples of differences in what’s morally relevant, which I would consider a values shift (e.g. consent in sexual ethics becoming important/the central moral fact)
  • Some interesting examples of values emerging from new conditions, e.g. privacy.
  • In general, generalising massively, the enablers of values change seem like changes in material conditions, power shifts towards new elites, some amount of intellectuals sharing takes. Unclear what proportion of credit to assign to what.

Moral progress looks good to me so far

…but then I would say that. And notice:

  • Lots of moral values I endorse are controversial, even within the (v small!) reference class of “WEIRD metropolitan elite with degrees in philosophy”
  • C.f. separateness of persons arguments, critiques of impartiality
  • Same for ways of reasoning
  • Cf critiques of means-end reasoning/instrumentalising everything
  • Philpapers moral views survey with \~⅓ splits to everything
  • Though these are on moral theories. They might endorse similar takeways, e.g. “slavery is wrong”, and even more granular ones, like “XYZ are usually the most morally relevant factors”
  • So maybe: the controversy is at the level of theory/justification, but there's more convergence at the level of first-order judgments and at the level of "was that historical change good."
  • Kantians think this convergence is no accident, that universalisability and impartiality fall out of the structure of reasoning.

  • Improved consistency/reasoning, maybe?
  • Less cope
  • Truer to your stated values
    • Though: sometimes cope is doing important work holding up illegible things you care about, defending from totalisation, etc.
  • Epistemic updates
  • Modulo infohazards, if those are actually real

It’s fine to be nervous about waving a wand/pressing the values change button

  • Change is fast
  • And slow change is generally better, more room to correct or abort
  • And our psychologies are fragile and respond better to slow changes
  • And, distinguishing the general “values change is usually good” to “this particular process is one you should submit to”
  • Also, it would be fine if we just had bad intuitions here
  • We often do
  • Loss aversion, personal attachments, etc
