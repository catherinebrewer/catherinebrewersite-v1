***Summary:** Within a set of reasonable cause areas, in order to maximise impact, should you follow excitement and personal fit (View 1), or prioritize by expected value even when that means less interesting work (View 2)? I sketch a few arguments for each, then navel-gaze and feel uncertain. I wouldn’t recommend reading unless you’re interested in “personal, meandering first drafts”.*

## **Two views on impact** {#two-views-on-impact}

Here's two views on impact, mildly exaggerated.

**View One**. When choosing what to work on, you should first narrow your set of considered causes to those which look good under scope-sensitive impartial altruistic perspectives. (To quickly approximate this set, you might like to think about the set of all topics that people in Constellation/EA orgs/etc work on.) Once you've done this, your career choice should be majorly driven by your own personal fit and any outstanding opportunities open to you, rather than trying to continue to prioritise between these areas by their relative importance. People are most impactful when they do this individually-driven thing and think about their relative excitement, interest, and any other rare qualities they possess, rather than trying to further prioritise by all-things-considered importance.

**View Two**. Your career choice should be majorly driven by your assessment of the marginal returns to working on some cause area (which you might try to quickly approximate by thinking about their importance/neglectedness/tractability, or using other heuristics). Personal fit, etc, will obviously play some part in this process — you'll probably be better at things you like than things you dislike, and so be more impactful doing them — but not the predominant one – you might love something less impactful than something else you’re merely okay with. You should be wary of surprising and suspicious convergence between what you like most and what's most impactful: some impact is about sucking it up in order to do the higher EV thing.

The second view is more sympathetic to 'much labour is fungible’-type thinking, where you ask about where it'd be best for e.g. a young generalist to go and work and expect the answer to be sensible; it's more sceptical of self-delusion, closer to viewing doing good as the kind of thing that often involves sacrifice. The first view is more interested in people as particular individuals, more willing to work on things because they seem interesting and enjoyable.

The second view, when pressed, thinks the first view is self-indulgent, sometimes delusional, susceptible to cope; the second view points out how morally serious and hard-nosed and [actually trying](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/HxXb8ogQSLDwnL7xh/notes-on-actually-trying) it is. The first view, when pressed, thinks the second view misses out on impact by thinking of people's labour too much as fungible units; it thinks it tends towards self-martyrdom; and it thinks it's too confident in its inside view. The first view views itself as appropriately epistemically modest, pragmatic, avoiding the failure mode of sacrificing overall good for legible smaller wins.

Each view has arguments to recommend it, most people follow some combination of both, and in practice the sketches I've given above are more like caricatures than accurate portraits. Disclaimers over, I want to talk a little more about these two views.

## **Hashing it out** {#hashing-it-out}

What’s the case for view 1? Three main reasons, I think:

1. [The persistence and intractability of cause prioritisation disagreements](#cause-prioritisation-disagreements)  
2. [Caring about something is necessary for being excellent at it](#caring-and-excellence)  
3. [Predicting the future is hard](#predicting-the-future-is-hard-[stub])

And, against these reasons (and for view 2):

1. [Disagreement alone is insufficient to suspend judgement](?tab=t.0#bookmark=id.lox8rm2i4wss)  
2. [Everything is like everything else, and on being the top 5%](?tab=t.0#bookmark=id.wy30l235yssb)  
3. [Motivated reasoning and surprising and suspicious convergence](#surprising-and-suspicious-convergence)

I'll say a bit more about each below.

### **Cause prioritisation disagreements** {#cause-prioritisation-disagreements}

Lots of smart, ambitious, scope-sensitive impartial altruists have thought for a long time about how to prioritise different work. Even when these people share the same starting point — roughly, if you care about the long-run future and think AI is plausibly going to be a very big deal, what should you do? — these people significantly disagree on what the most impactful work is to do. Two implications:

1. First, we should be more pessimistic about ourselves landing on the correct cause prioritisation answer and being confident in it (the epistemic update)  
2. Second, we should update towards thinking that these causes don't differ *wildly* in cost-effectiveness; if they did, then given we've already done some hard thinking on this, we'd expect more agreement on the relative importance of each cause (the empirical update)

(There's lots of room to disagree here of course: maybe we haven't done enough thinking, or the right kind of thinking, etc. I won't get into this here.)

Clearly, this argument alone isn't sufficient. (Compare: "Lots of economists disagree about which policies would boost GDP growth the most despite thinking hard about it, so all these policies must have similar effects on growth". These premises aren’t sufficient to establish the conclusion beyond reasonable doubt; we'd need to say more.) 

But I suspect people can do the right kind of "saying more" here, at least to make this argument give reason to adopt view 1\.

### **Caring and excellence** {#caring-and-excellence}

Most things are heavy-tailed; mere 1-10x differences in abstract importance can easily be outweighed by where someone is on the distribution of impact for their role; and caring about your role is a significant determinant of impact (perhaps necessary for being one of the best people in the world at it). So, caring about what you work on really matters. Hence view 1\.

The best two ways to reply, I think:

1. **Lots of work is kind of the same, and people care about properties not specifics**. People care more about properties of particular problems — e.g. "involves working closely in a team", or "has technical problem-solving" or "fast feedback loops" or "likeminded, value-aligned colleagues" or "wrangling with poorly defined concepts" or "involves communicating ideas" — than the hyper-specific details of each role working on a problem of that type. (So, empirical ML types would probably also enjoy software engineering, maybe economics, maybe startuppy work, depending on what properties of "empirical ML" they like; journalists would probably enjoy comms or grantmaking or being a civil servant etc; you get the idea.)  
2. **Being the best in the world at one thing is hard, but being the best thing in the world at the intersection of 2 or 3 things is much easier.** Sure, maybe loving your work gets you into the top 0.1%, [but you can probably get into the top \~5% by just trying hard](https://danluu.com/p95-skill/), and that's all you need to be world class at the intersection of two such things.

### **Predicting the future is hard \[stub\]** {#predicting-the-future-is-hard-[stub]}

This is like: even if you think there's consensus among some people on cause areas (e.g. one should work on AI control rather than AI welfare, in general), the future is messy, people are bad at predicting it, and this still seems like it could easily be false. But in contrast (so the argument goes), you're probably right about what you like, dislike, and are especially suited for. So given this set of plausibly impactful opportunities, use your personal skills and interests to prioritise more than you use predictions.

### **Surprising and suspicious convergence** {#surprising-and-suspicious-convergence}

I think the main argument against view 1 is just: it would be [so surprising](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/omoZDu8ScNbot6kXS/beware-surprising-and-suspicious-convergence) if the most impactful thing to do was also the most interesting and exciting\!

You're a person who cares about all kinds of different things. We care about our friends' opinions and our job titles and prestige and location and, more abstractly, some things are just richer and more interesting to us than other things. We can [learn to pay attention](https://www.gleech.org/codes), but we have these preferences. And it would be so weird if they lined up with impact — what mechanism would ensure that?

So we have this "surprisingness" argument: it would be surprising if these coincided. And we have an explanation (motivated reasoning) for why we might mistakenly think they coincide. And we have the stakes – there are heavy tails everywhere. These choices really matter.

### **Misc other notes** {#misc-other-notes}

I don't really know what I think here. Some notes:

* The way I've written view 2 so far has been with a framing of "sacrifice", but that's not the only available framing, and not clearly the most truthful one. Another frame is: this is just ordinary trade-offs – it's not quite as trivial a tradeoff as "I eat my vegetables rather than eating cake", and it's also not quite the same as reflective vs instinctual desires, which people sometimes like to analogise this to: I think I endorse wanting to do interesting work, and don’t feel any higher-order pull to have a different desire. But nonetheless, I think there's some "ordinary tradeoffs" framing that works. And when I put it like that, I feel closer to — yeah, wow, I got lucky that I can make these calls. And my whole life will just be choosing, and this is just how it feels to make choices; I'm finally the self-determined agent.  
* And sometimes I feel like: look, let's all be honest with each other, view 1 is clearly false. "Follow your excitement" is a fine policy to have, but it won't always be the most impactful one. Sometimes you do just need to look clear-eyed at the thing, and suck it up, and be realistic about tradeoffs. We should stop pretending.  
* At other times, when I'm feeling more reflective and/or full of cope, I think about how confused I still feel about how to make things go well, and how confused I think we all are, and wonder: hmm, yeah, maybe there's something to view 1; we're on such shaky footing; and maybe we'll look further and do better if it feels totally pure and uncoercive. Not "no tradeoffs" in the fake idealistic sense, but no tradeoffs like: the way it feels when you're awake and alive and can do anything. Embarking on a voyage, with something like honesty in your heart.  
  * (But can't you honestly make these tradeoffs and optimise for impact?)  
  * (Maybe the actual thing I object to is not being honest about these tradeoffs — like, don't optimise for impact only a bit, but then call yourself... what? an EA? Nope, I *don't* believe that. So maybe I have some silly hangups here. Like, “you can sacrifice impact if you want, but you should feel vaguely bad about it all the time” – do I actually believe that? Clearly not. But, but, it feels important that view 2 is closer to the truth, and I want people to act on it…)
