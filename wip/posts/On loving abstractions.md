

## On loving abstractions

I read a blog post recently where the author had been trying to cure her misanthropic tendencies. She'd been reading classic books, and was struck by their wisdom and their cynicism: she realised she didn't want to fall into the same trap. So, to reaffirm her love for the common man, to feel her love for humanity, she went to a philosophy meetup. She was horrified. These people should, in theory, be similar to her — they're earnestly discussing philosophy in their free time — and they're miles off it. They weren't truth-seeking, or otherwise doing this act well. The only joy she can find is in trying to play their game, in a kind of cool contemptuous way. She leaves the meetup more jaded than she started.

  

I worry about this kind of thing. When I reflect on my values, something central and important is wanting all moral patients to flourish. This can sound twee or idealistic or arrogant: I don't really feel it that way. It feels true, and central, and surprisingly visceral. I've wept over this thing and been moved to grindsetposting over it. And this feeling — which is so visceral when directed at humanity in the aggregate, one great shapeless blob — becomes pretend, thin and dodgy, when I try to direct it at specific people. Maybe through social ineptitude, from being unskilful, not knowing how to deepen the conversation, whatever: I often feel dissatisfied when I leave my social bubble. (I think of a massage class I went to recently, with a really earnest young woman taking notes on the chakras.) It's not disdainful and it's not contempt, and I don't think it's the same as introversion or awkwardness either — it's more like, I can't whole-heartedly will you to succeed with your projects, I can't join in the chorus for you. I can only love the abstraction, not the particular instances of it.

  

Okay, well, what's so wrong with loving an abstraction? I can think of a few things. 

  

First, it's faker. "Loving humanity" is easier, lower friction, than loving a particular human. But there's something slippery and vague about loving humanity if you can't love its constituents — it's a nice compression, and compression has its purposes, but it should connect to something real. An abstraction is perhaps less tug-at-the-heartstrings, which can be good, but also makes it easier to trick yourself, convince yourself you love it. And so if you only love an abstraction, you're probably tricking yourself somewhere. (Another way of putting this: loving abstractions feels rote, formulaic. But remember, the reason we loved the abstraction was for its contents. And if you can't love the content, you're wrong and shouldn't love the abstraction. Map vs territory, etc, you should love the essence and truth of the thing.)

  

Similarly, intuitively, it's just a false claim. If I claim to love a set, and love it in virtue of each individual member, then I should love the members, right? That's what makes the claim true. There's something tethering and grounded about loving these individuals. Similarly, being able to bump into a particular person and love them is a check on the truth of your claim to loving humanity.

  

Are there ways I might be overdoing this? Is loving abstractions fine?

  

Maybe I'm just conflating loving someone with respecting them, or liking their company. After all, you can love someone — wish them well, feel warmly towards them, hope they achieve their goals — and not want to spend much time with them; that's life. And that's fine, you don't have to like everyone.

  

But I don't think that's what I'm worried about, exactly. My worry is more that the emotions I risk feeling here aren't just boredom or neutrality, but a kind of alienation, of huge otherness. ("How can that be your goal? Is that really who you want to be?") I think that kind of alienation can metastasise into contempt, and that contempt is corrosive to love. And if you can't love particular people, because of this alienation, then claiming to love humanity feels uncomfortably close to a lie.

  

Maybe, let's try another angle, I'm conflating love-as-feeling with love-as-attitude. Love-as-feeling might be hard to command; it's difficult to will oneself into feeling differently. But that could be okay, because what we care about is my values, what I believe and what guides my actions. This is just a psychological issue: humans have quirks in their psychology or emotions they don't endorse all the time. It might be discomforting that your emotions don't match your values, but it doesn't mean your values are fake, or that you're a hypocrite.

  

 I'm most sympathetic to this argument, but it seems striking that I'm emotionally in conflict with a very strongly held value of mine. (And maybe other people are too?) It would be good to figure out how to love the thing as well as the abstraction.

  

Let me try to describe what my worry isn’t. My issue isn't failing to love people because they're bad at some X I care about (see our blogger, who couldn’t love those amateur philosophers who were so bad at truth-seeking). It's fine that many people aren't good at things I value, and it'd be strange if they were all good at it. 

  

My issue is closer to failing to love people because they don't value thing X I intensely care about. It's not quite that — I think this brings to mind, for example, struggling to love people if they aren't committed to the truth, or to being a more moral person, but those are both narrower than the X I have in mind. What I have in mind feels a bit broader: it's more like where the X is "having good projects" or "living well" or something. But if people don't value the ends that I see as ends to themselves, or don't seem to recognise things that I see as essential to living well, it doesn't feel like I have many moves left. 

One move you can make, in these cases, is to love people in a sort of patronising or condescending way. We can break this down into two kinds of different ways to be patronising. I think one is the way that adults might love small children. We know that their current goals are not their end goals, and that they'll change their mind. We still love them; we want them to achieve their current goals, whilst also wanting their goals to change in the fullness of time. 

 Another way to love is to love the way humans love pets. Maybe we think their goals are not the noblest, but they're not going to change, and we love them anyway. We don't treat them in the same way that we treat adults, or even small children.

  

There's a dilemma here. Loving people in the second kind of patronising way doesn't feel like love, or at least isn't the kind of version I want to have and value having. I don't want to relate to people around me the way I relate to way less developed agents; it would be patronising, it would be condescending, it would be unfounded, I think, and pretty profoundly cruel.

  

The former way of loving people in this patronising way, that you might love a child, wanting them to achieve their current goals, wanting them to get better goals, loving them in the knowledge that their goals will one day be better, also seems to me importantly sneaky.I worry that it’s implicitly appealing to some kind of idealisation. Something like: "Oh, I love these people and I want them to flourish. By flourishing, I don't just mean all these trivial desires X, Y, Z — no, these people are going to love something else when they flourish. That's what flourishing is, and of course I want that for them!"

  

Why is that sneaky? Well, a few reasons. One is that when I think about particular instances of people, they are in fact often pursuing projects XYZ, and my guess is that if you offered them some trade to some more approved-by-Catherine-flourishing-goal, they'd say no. 

  

The second is that this kind of idealised love comes close to the appeal of "maybe you'd like them once they flourished?", and I think that love is fake and false. Those idealised people are alarmingly distant: who would I be loving then? I don’t want to love idealisations, that’s another abstraction. I want to love real people. 

  

The final reason is that it’s not even clear that I endorse wanting them to change their personal projects! I'm in a bind here. On a pretty deep level, somewhere in my values, I feel committed to giving people lots of autonomy and freedom and agency over their projects. But more viscerally, on a gut level, I can't help but feel that some projects are just smaller, not worth spending your life on, some projects just don't matter. And then it's hard to approach those projects with equality and love in mind.

  

 I'm not sure what to do here, or where this leaves me. I begin by thinking that this is a skill issue on my part, and that there are ways to cultivate attention and openness to other people, to in time, let them reveal the qualities, goals, and textures of their lives, which I find extremely easy on a gut level to really love. That's my hope, at least.

  

My worry is that loving people may require a kind of epistemic humility about the good, an openness to being wrong, to avoid sliding into paternalism, and also to avoid endorsing bad fake projects that you don't genuinely believe in. If that's the case, I don't know that I'm going to be able to maintain this kind of humility. (This is obviously a very arrogant thing to say, and I hope I'm almost universally less grandiose. I think at almost every other avenue in my life, I'm not a figure to respect or look up to: certainly I don't feel like I'm doing a good job of living up to the standards of my personal projects.) 

  

In my heart, I just do think there are some personal projects that are worth more than others. This isn't just an EA totalising thing — even before then, I thought that some kinds of beauty or truth or virtues are more worth pursuing than other projects. I think that now, too.) If loving humanity, not loving abstractions, requires me to overthrow this belief, I'm not sure where to go next.
